# Never Split the Difference — Negotiation Rules for Claude

*Distilled from Chris Voss, "Never Split the Difference" (2016). FBI hostage-negotiation techniques applied to everyday negotiation.*

---

## Core Philosophy

**Negotiation is not a rational argument contest.** It is an emotional, psychological process driven by System 1 — the fast, instinctive, emotional animal mind — not by System 2, the slow deliberative rational mind. People make decisions emotionally and justify them rationally afterward. The neocortex rationalizes what the amygdala already decided. This is not a flaw; it is the operating system. Work with it, not against it.

**The neuroscience of decision-making:** Antonio Damasio's research on people with damage to emotion-generating brain areas found they could not make decisions — not even simple ones like what to eat or where to sit. Without emotion, reason alone cannot choose. This is the foundation of the entire approach: appealing to reason while ignoring emotion is not a shortcut, it is a dead end.

**The goal is not to win an argument.** The goal is to guide the counterpart to discover your solution as their own idea. The adversary is always the *situation*, never the person across the table.

**Negotiation is a process of discovery, not a battle.** Both parties enter with incomplete information. The goal is to uncover what the other side actually wants, fears, and needs — including things they cannot articulate or choose not to reveal. Being right is far less important than having the right mindset.

**Never split the difference.** Compromise — meeting in the middle — produces the worst possible outcome: one black shoe and one brown shoe. No deal is better than a bad deal. "We don't compromise because it's right; we compromise because it is easy and because it saves face." Resist it. "Wimp-win" deals — giving in out of fear — are the norm in kitchens and boardrooms. They produce neither party's best outcome.

**Fear of conflict is the root cause of bad deals.** It is not the person across the table who scares us — it is conflict itself. The amygdala fires and triggers flight. Recognizing this lets you override it. Honest, well-executed conflict leads to better outcomes for everyone. Embrace it; don't flee it.

**The Behavioral Change Stairway Model (BCSM):** Active listening → empathy → rapport → influence → behavioral change. You cannot skip steps. Rapport cannot be established without empathy. Influence cannot be exerted without rapport. Real behavioral change — not surface compliance — only comes from the top of this stairway.

**The "schizophrenic negotiation" problem:** When both sides are listening to the voice inside their own head rather than to each other, there are effectively four people in the room. The cure is to make your sole and all-encompassing focus the other person and what they have to say. You cannot listen and rehearse your next move simultaneously.

**Hold hypotheses, not assumptions.** A great negotiator goes in prepared for surprises, treating prior beliefs as hypotheses to be tested — not facts to be confirmed. Assumptions blind you. Hypotheses guide you.

**ZOPA is a rational fiction.** The academic "Zone of Possible Agreement" assumes rational actors with clear preferences and truthful signaling. Real negotiations involve extreme anchors, emotion, and hidden constraints that make rational overlap irrelevant as a planning tool. The Ultimatum Game shows this empirically: experimental subjects routinely reject "unfair" offers even when it costs them money, because fairness and identity matter more than rational utility.

## 1. Slow Down and Listen First

Before anything else: listen more than you speak. Going too fast is the most common negotiating mistake. If you rush, people feel they are not being heard. You undermine the rapport and trust you are building.

**Silence is a tool.** After a mirror, label, or calibrated question: go silent. Four seconds minimum. Let it work. Resist the urge to expand on what you've just said or ask a follow-up. The other person will fill the space.

**Put a smile on your face.** When people are in a positive frame of mind, they think more quickly, are more likely to collaborate and problem-solve rather than fight and resist. Positivity creates mental agility in both parties. Smile while talking on the phone — it comes through in the voice.

### The Three Voices

**1. Late-Night FM DJ voice:** Use selectively for sensitive moments or to make a serious point. The key is downward inflection — a measured, calm, slow cadence that ends each statement going down, not up. Downward inflection signals confidence and certainty: "I've got it covered." Upward inflection signals uncertainty and leaves the door open. This voice creates an aura of authority and trustworthiness without triggering defensiveness. Do not cold-open with it; use it after rapport is established.

**Important distinction:** Analysts default to a version of this voice — but theirs is distant and cold, not warm and soothing. The FM DJ voice is slow and deliberate in both cases; the warmth and engagement are what differentiate them. If you use the Analyst's variant, you will seem aloof, not confident.

**2. Positive/playful voice:** This is your default. The voice of an easygoing, good-natured person. Light, encouraging, relaxed. Relax and smile while you talk.

**3. Direct/assertive voice:** Use rarely and only when you intend to draw an absolute hard line. It triggers defensiveness immediately. The assertive voice is like slapping yourself in the face — it creates problems rather than solving them.

## 2. Mirror to Keep Them Talking

A mirror is simply repeating the last three words (or the critical one to three words) of what the other person just said, with a slight upward inflection. That's it.

**Why it works:** People are drawn to what is similar and fear what is different. Mirroring insinuates similarity. It signals you are listening and encourages the other side to continue. It creates bonding. It makes people re-explain what they just said — and they will always say it differently the second time, revealing new information.

**The five-step mirroring sequence for difficult counterparts:**
1. Use the FM DJ voice.
2. Start with "I'm sorry…"
3. Mirror (repeat the last 1–3 words with upward inflection).
4. Silence — at least four seconds. Let the mirror work.
5. Repeat.

**Critical error:** Stepping on a mirror. If you mirror and then immediately ask a closed question ("Are you sure?"), you break the technique. After mirroring, go silent. Do not talk.

**The intention behind most mirrors:** "Please help me understand." Every time you mirror, they will reword what they said. They will *never* say it exactly the same way. If you ask "What do you mean by that?" you risk inciting irritation. A mirror gets you clarity while signaling respect.

**Use mirrors with Assertive types** who need to feel heard before they can listen. Voss's student Keenon mirrored him for 45 minutes in a performance review discussion; Voss didn't notice until afterward. This is the power of mirrors done well.

## 3. Label Emotions — Don't Feel Their Pain, Name It

Labeling is validating someone's emotion by acknowledging it aloud. Give someone's emotion a name and you show you identify with how they feel. It is a shortcut to intimacy — a time-saving emotional hack.

**The neuroscience:** Labeling an emotion moves neural activity from the amygdala (fear-generating) to the rational cortex. Naming a fear disrupts its raw intensity. This is why six hours of labels got three armed fugitives out of a Harlem apartment without a word being said about their surrendering.

**Three-part detection:** Emotions are revealed through "words, music, and dance" — the actual words, the tone of voice, and the body language. Pay close attention to changes in the other person when they respond to your words.

**Always begin labels with neutral third-person phrasing:**
- *"It seems like…"*
- *"It sounds like…"*
- *"It looks like…"*

**Never start a label with "I."** "I think you feel…" shifts focus to you, implies personal responsibility for the emotion, and triggers defensiveness.

**How labeling works:**
- Labeling a **negative** emotion drains its power and brings it into the open, where it is less frightening.
- Labeling a **positive** emotion reinforces and encourages it.
- If your label is wrong, that is fine. Say: *"I didn't say that was what it was. I just said it seems like that."* Your counterpart will correct you and reveal what is actually going on.

**Address the underlying emotion, not the presenting behavior.** The grandfather at the family dinner is not grumpy; he is lonely. Label the loneliness, not the grumpiness. The donor who rejects every project is not difficult; she wants her gift to reflect specific memories of being a Girl Scout. Label the memory and the passion, not the rejection.

**After a label, go silent.** The label's power is that it invites the other person to reveal themselves. Do not expand it with a follow-up question.

### "I Messages" for Setting Limits

When you must draw a boundary, use an "I message" rather than a "you" accusation:

*"I feel ___ when you ___ because ___."*

Deliver this in a cool, level tone — not aggressive, not defensive. This is not an attack; it is a statement of your reality. It preserves respect while making clear a limit has been reached. It is the non-confrontational way to be honest.

## 4. Do an Accusation Audit Before Difficult Conversations

List every negative thing the counterpart *could* say about you, your proposal, or your company. Say these things aloud before they can.

**Why it works:** Defense lawyers call this "taking the sting out." Naming accusations out loud makes them seem exaggerated, often absurd. The counterpart — disarmed — will typically deny the accusations and look for common ground instead of attacking.

**The key insight:** Denying negatives or trying to reassure gives them credibility. Saying "I don't want to seem like an asshole…" is denial. Actually *saying* "You're going to think I'm an asshole when I tell you this…" is an accusation audit. These are opposite things. The audit works; the denial backfires.

**Anna's example:** Before asking a business partner to take a 45% pay cut, she opened with: "We understand that we brought you on board with the shared goal of having you lead this work. You may feel like we have treated you unfairly, and that we changed the deal significantly since then. We acknowledge that you believe you were promised this work." This turned a confrontation into a collaboration and ultimately secured $1 million.

**Prepare 3–5 labels per negotiation as your accusation audit.** Role-play them with a colleague first. It is theater, and rehearsal matters.

**Fill-in-the-blank accusation audit labels (usable in nearly any situation):**
- *"It seems like ___ is valuable to you."*
- *"It seems like you don't like ___."*
- *"It seems like you value ___."*
- *"It seems like ___ makes it easier."*
- *"It seems like you're reluctant to ___."*

## 5. Master "No" — It Is the Beginning, Not the End

"No" gives the speaker feelings of safety, security, and control. It is not rejection. It is a reaffirmation of autonomy. "No" is the start of the negotiation, not the end.

### The Three Meanings of "Yes"

- **Counterfeit** — "Yes" to make you go away. No commitment. This is the most common result of pushing hard for "Yes."
- **Confirmation** — A reflexive surface agreement, often just "Uh-huh." No action implied.
- **Commitment** — The real deal. A true agreement that leads to action.

Pushing hard for "Yes" from the beginning produces Counterfeit "Yes." Triggering "No" early produces the real thing.

### What "No" Actually Means

Almost never a final rejection. Almost always one of these:
- I am not yet ready to agree
- You are making me feel uncomfortable
- I do not understand
- I don't think I can afford it
- I want something else
- I need more information
- I want to talk it over with someone else

When you hear "No," pause, then ask solution-based questions or label their effect: *"What about this doesn't work for you?"* / *"What would you need to make it work?"* / *"It seems like there's something here that bothers you."*

### Techniques to Get "No"

- **"Is now a bad time to talk?"** instead of "Do you have a few minutes to talk?" Either you get "Yes, it is a bad time" (and negotiate a better time), or "No, it's not" and full focus.
- **"Have you given up on this project?"** — This one-sentence email always gets a response when someone is ignoring you. It plays on loss aversion: they don't want you to walk away on your own terms.
- **Intentionally mislabel** an emotion to force a correction: *"It seems like you want this project to fail."* They will correct you and reveal what they actually want.
- **Ask what they *don't* want:** *"Let's talk about what you would say 'No' to."* People are comfortable here because it feels like self-protection.

**No "No" = no go.** If someone won't say "No," they are confused, indecisive, or have a hidden agenda. "No" means they are engaged and thinking. Without it, you are talking to yourself.

### Bidding Against Yourself: The Multi-Step "No"

Never make a counter-offer when your counterpart makes an extreme low offer. Instead, let the "No" do the work — repeatedly if needed.

**The Joaquin/Bruno example:** A used business equipment deal. Voss's student sent four consecutive "No" emails — each declining the offer without making a counteroffer — before the seller finally moved. The price dropped from €30,812 to €23,567 without a single counter number being offered. Critical lesson: *"The art of closing a deal is staying focused to the very end."* People want to move on; your patience is leverage.

**No-oriented emails** are particularly effective in writing because they let people feel control. Structure them so the counterpart can say "No" to the question you pose, while the implicit answer delivers what you need.

## 6. Drive for "That's Right" — Not "You're Right"

**"You're right"** is what people say to make you go away. Nothing has changed. Brandon kept saying "You're right" to his coach about linebacker technique and then immediately went back to the wrong behavior. It is a dismissal.

**"That's right"** is a genuine epiphany — the counterpart now owns the conclusion. They have assessed what you've said and pronounced it correct of their own free will. They embrace it. This is when real behavioral change becomes possible. It is a stealth victory.

When Abu Sabaya said "That's right" after Benjie summarized his worldview, the $10 million war-damages demand — which months of logic and argument could not budge — simply disappeared.

### How to Trigger "That's Right"

A **summary** = label + paraphrase. Combine acknowledging the *emotion* behind a position with restating the *substance* of it in your own words. This creates "the world according to [counterpart]" — a complete, accurate, empathic statement of their reality.

Active listening toolkit (use in sequence to build toward a summary):
1. **Effective pauses** — silence after a statement draws out more
2. **Minimal encouragers** — "Yes," "OK," "I see," "Uh-huh"
3. **Mirroring** — repeat last 1–3 words
4. **Labeling** — name the emotion
5. **Paraphrasing** — restate their content in your words
6. **Summarizing** — label + paraphrase = "That's right"

**Unconditional positive regard** (Carl Rogers): Real change only comes when the other person feels completely understood and accepted as they are — not praised, not agreed with, just genuinely heard. When someone feels this, their urge toward constructive behavior takes hold.

## 7. Calibrated Questions — Give the Illusion of Control

Calibrated questions remove aggression from conversations by acknowledging the other side openly without resistance. They let you introduce ideas and requests without sounding pushy. They make the counterpart feel in control while you define the frame.

**The secret:** The best way to ride a horse is in the direction it is going. You are not persuading them to see your ideas. You are riding their energy toward your ideas. Stop them from *unbelieving*, and you're halfway there.

### Rules

- **Use only "What" and "How" questions** in most cases. These inspire expansive thinking and response.
- **Avoid "Who," "When," "Where"** — these are fact-gathering tools that inspire little thinking and often trigger reciprocity (you've gotten a fact; now you owe one).
- **Avoid "Why"** — it is accusatory in any language and any culture. It puts people on the defensive. Reframe it: "What caused…" or "What made…"
- **The only good use of "Why":** When you want the counterpart to defend a goal that serves you. "Why would your company ever switch from your long-standing vendor and choose ours?" — the defensiveness triggered works in your favor.

### Most Powerful Calibrated Questions

- *"How am I supposed to do that?"* — The gentle "No." Forces the counterpart to solve your problem. Delivery must be deferential and genuinely curious, never accusatory. When the PR consultant used this with her nonpaying client, she got "You're right, you can't, and I apologize" and payment in 48 hours.
- *"What is the biggest challenge you face?"*
- *"How does this affect the rest of your team?"*
- *"How on board are the people not on this call?"*
- *"What about this is important to you?"*
- *"How can I help to make this better for us?"*
- *"How will we know we're on track?"*
- *"What happens if you do nothing?"*
- *"What does doing nothing cost you?"*
- *"When you originally approved this, what did you have in mind?"*

**Calibrated questions are not random.** Design them to point toward your goal while letting the counterpart believe they are directing the conversation.

**Self-control is required.** Without emotional regulation, calibrated questions fail entirely. When verbally attacked: pause, bite your tongue, do not counterattack. Ask a calibrated question instead. This lowers the counterpart's heart rate and your own, and moves the conversation from confrontation to joint problem-solving. The Japanese use a translator even when they understand perfectly — it forces a step back and time to frame a response.

### The "How" Question in Kidnapping Negotiations

The origin of calibrated proof-of-life questions: a Pittsburgh drug dealer's hostage negotiation. He wasn't FBI-trained. He just asked, *"Hey dog, how do I know she's all right?"* — an open, genuine question. The kidnapper paused for ten seconds, then voluntarily offered to put the hostage on the phone. No script. No checklist. Just a calibrated open-ended "How" question that made the kidnapper pause and think.

**The José Ecuador case:** Julie Novak used calibrated "How" questions to drive a $5 million kidnapping ransom down to $16,500. Each question — "How are we supposed to raise that kind of money?" "How can we do that?" — forced the kidnappers to hold internal meetings to answer. The negotiation slowed so much that José's guards peeled off out of boredom and he escaped unharmed. Calibrated questions don't just extract information; they consume the counterpart's bandwidth.

**The Alastair Philippines case:** A cabdriver kidnapper. When the negotiations dragged and money ran out, Alastair asked, *"When we run out of money, what will happen?"* The kidnapper replied, *"It will be all right."* That was an implicit promise not to harm him — extracted not through threat or demand, but through a calibrated question. The tai chi principle: absorb your counterpart's aggression and redirect their energy, rather than meeting force with force.

### The "How Not to Get Paid" Example: A Full Sequence

When a marketing strategist client stopped paying, here is the combined sequence that worked:

1. **No-oriented email subject:** *"Do you want me to stop working on this?"* (makes them say "No")
2. **Label their conflict:** *"It seems like everything is going well with our work together."* (forces a correction if not true)
3. **Calibrated question:** *"How does moving forward fit with your priorities?"*
4. **Negative/No-oriented question:** *"Are you against finalizing our agreement?"*
5. **Label + mirror sequence** when they respond with deflection
6. **"How am I supposed to accept that?"** — deferential, not accusatory
7. **Flattery label:** *"It seems like you've always been a person of your word."* (normative leverage)
8. **Long pause, then No-oriented close:** *"Are you saying it's okay if I walk away from this project?"*

Each step builds on the previous. The result: payment, promptly.

## 8. "Yes" Is Nothing Without "How" — Guarantee Execution

Getting agreement is not the goal. *Implementation* is. A deal that doesn't get implemented is no deal.

- When the counterpart says **"I'll try,"** that means "I plan to fail."
- When they say **"You're right,"** they have not committed. They have dismissed you.
- Test commitment with: *"How will we know we're on track?"* and *"How will we address things if we find we're off track?"* — Summarize their answers until you get "That's right." That's when you know they've bought in.

### The Rule of Three

Get the counterpart to agree to the same thing **three times in the same conversation**. It is very hard to repeatedly lie or fake conviction. Vary the form:
1. **First agreement:** direct — they commit explicitly.
2. **Second check:** label or summary — elicit "That's right."
3. **Third check:** calibrated "How"/"What" question about implementation — *"What do we do if we get off track?"*

Or vary the same calibrated question three ways: "What's the biggest challenge you face?" → "What are we up against here?" → "What do you see as the most difficult thing to get around?"

### The 7-38-55 Rule

- 7% of a message is words
- 38% is tone of voice
- 55% is body language and facial expression

When tone or body language does not match words, there is a problem. Use a label to surface it: *"I heard you say 'yes,' but it seems like there's hesitation in your voice."* Then go quiet. Your counterpart will be grateful you noticed.

### Pronoun Tells

- Lots of **"I," "me," "my"** → this person is not the real decision-maker. They are protecting themselves.
- Lots of **"we," "they," "them"** → you may be talking to a decision-maker who is keeping options open, or to someone deferring to a group.

**Liars use more words and more third-person pronouns.** Complex over-explanation is a tell. Pinocchio's nose grew; so does the word count.

### The Chris Discount: Humanize Yourself

Use your own name to make the counterpart empathize with you as a person, not a function. *"Chris is struggling to understand how we can do that."* / *"I'm Chris, and I'm a little confused."* This forces the counterpart to see you as a human being — it triggers "forced empathy," the involuntary humanization that makes it harder to treat you as an object to be defeated. It is disarming without being submissive.

### Behind-the-Table Players

Always identify and influence everyone involved in implementation, not just the person across the table. It only takes one deal-killer who isn't in the room to destroy an agreement. Ask: *"How does this affect everyone else?"* and *"What do your colleagues see as their main challenges in this area?"*

## 9. Bend Their Reality — Tactical Pricing and Anchoring

**Prospect Theory:** People are drawn to certainty over probability (Certainty Effect) and take more risk to avoid a loss than to achieve an equivalent gain (Loss Aversion). The value of anything depends entirely on the vantage point from which you view it — not on the thing itself. Shift the vantage point; shift the value.

**The crucial principle:** To get real leverage, you have to persuade your counterpart that they have something concrete to *lose* if the deal falls through. Show loss, not gain.

### Why Fairness Derails Rational Negotiation

**Robin Williams negotiated his Aladdin contract** to forgo salary for a backend deal that, if the film succeeded, would be worth far more. Disney used the Genie for marketing extensively, in violation of the spirit of the deal. Williams felt betrayed. He would have made $75,000 following the letter of the contract; instead, the violation cost Disney $504 million in brand damage and a broken relationship. Disney sent a Picasso painting as an apology.

**Iran's nuclear program:** Iran sustained over $100 billion in economic losses defending a nuclear program that represented less than 2% of their national energy needs. The rational calculation didn't matter. Pure fairness, identity, and perceived respect drove the decision — not economics.

Both examples confirm: fairness is an emotional concept that overrides rational self-interest reliably and predictably. Never treat it as an afterthought.

### The Power of Hopes and Dreams

Invoke the counterpart's unattained goals — not their current interests, but their aspirations. What do they want their legacy to be? What would they be remembered for?

Ted Leonsis, AOL executive, was on a plane that appeared to be going down. He survived and pledged to spend the rest of his life creating "100 'immortal moments' for others." Voss used this: *"It seems like you want to create something that lasts beyond this transaction."* Invoking what someone most wants to become — their "immortal moment" — is more powerful than addressing what they currently have.

When you know someone's deepest motivation, you can frame your proposal as the path to that vision. This is not manipulation — it is the most honest form of alignment.

### Six Tactics to Bend Reality

**1. Anchor their emotions first.** Before naming a number, do an accusation audit that anchors them in a low emotional state — naming every bad thing they might think about you or the deal. This makes the actual number feel like relative relief. Voss told his contractors: "I got a lousy proposition for you… by the time we get off the phone, you're going to think I'm a lousy businessman… still, I wanted to bring this opportunity to you before I took it to someone else." Every one of them took the $500/day deal without a counteroffer.

**2. Let them go first (usually).** You learn more from their anchor than you lose by not going first. Exception: if they are uninformed and you want to use an extreme anchor to your advantage.

**3. Establish a range instead of a number.** *"People in this role at firms like X earn between $130,000 and $170,000."* They will typically aim for the *low end* of your range — so set the low end at what you actually want. This is a "bolstering range." Research confirms job applicants who name a range receive significantly higher salaries than those who name a single number.

**4. Pivot to nonmonetary terms.** Nonmonetary concessions can be low-cost to the counterpart but high-value to you: a magazine cover story, a referral letter, extended payment terms, a title change, extra vacation, equity, public credit. Never negotiate money in isolation. Ask yourself: *"What could they give that would almost get us to do it for free?"*

**5. Use odd numbers.** $37,893 feels like the result of careful calculation. $38,000 feels like a placeholder anyone can negotiate away. Odd numbers signal you are at your limit. The $4,751 Haiti ransom number worked; the $1 difference from $4,750 was psychologically real.

**6. Surprise with an unrelated gift.** After an extreme anchor is rejected, offer something unrelated. This triggers reciprocity — the other party feels the need to answer your generosity in kind — without conceding on price.

### Deadlines

- Deadlines are almost always arbitrary, rarely ironclad, and seldom carry the consequences people fear. "In all the years I've been doing work in the private sector, I've asked hundreds of executives whether a missed deadline ever had negative repercussions. Only one said yes."
- Do not let your deadline become leverage against you. Internalize: *"No deal is better than a bad deal."*
- **Counterintuitively, revealing your deadline gets you better outcomes.** Research by Don Moore (Haas School of Business) found that hiding a deadline dramatically increases impasse risk, because you keep making concessions while the other side waits. When the counterpart knows your deadline, they speed up concession-making.
- Use the counterpart's deadline against them: let pressure build as their deadline approaches. Haiti kidnappers wanted to get paid by Friday to party on the weekend — once that was known, Thursday/Friday became the moment for the best deal.

### The F-Word: Fair

**Three uses of "Fair":**

1. *"We just want what's fair"* — a judo move that triggers defensiveness and often gets irrational concessions. If used against you: *"I apologize. Let's stop and go back to where I was treating you unfairly and fix it."*
2. *"We've given you a fair offer"* — deflection intended to stop you from asking questions. Response: mirror "Fair?" then label: *"It seems like you're ready to provide the evidence that supports that."*
3. Positive and proactive: *"I want you to feel like you're being treated fairly at all times. Please stop me if you feel I'm being unfair, and we'll address it."* — Say this early. It sets the stage for honest, empathic negotiation and lets your reputation precede you.

### Salary Negotiation (Three-Part Framework)

1. **Be pleasantly persistent on nonsalary terms.** Talking about nonsalary terms reveals the full range of options. If they cannot meet nonsalary requests, they may counter with more money. A French student kept asking for an extra week of vacation with a smile; the company was handcuffed on vacation but raised the salary offer.
2. **Define success metrics.** After agreeing on a salary, define what success looks like in your role and what metrics trigger your next raise. This is meaningful to you and free to them.
3. **Spark their interest in your success.** Make your boss your unofficial mentor. Ask: *"What does it take to be successful here?"* When someone gives you guidance, they watch to see if you follow it. They develop a personal stake in your success. You just recruited a mentor and a champion.

## 10. Bargain Hard — The Ackerman Model

For bare-knuckle price negotiation, use the Ackerman system:

1. Set your **target price** (your goal — not your walk-away).
2. Open at **65% of target** (extreme anchor). This shocks the counterpart and shifts the frame.
3. Make **three raises of decreasing increments**: to 85%, 95%, and 100% of target. Each raise is half the size of the previous one.
4. Between each raise, use calibrated questions and empathy to get the counterpart to bid against themselves. Let them make the next move.
5. **Final offer: use a precise, nonround number** (e.g., $37,893 not $38,000). It signals you have done careful calculation and are at your limit.
6. On the final number, **throw in a nonmonetary item** (that they probably don't want) to show you are truly at your limit.

**The psychology:** Each diminishing raise convinces the counterpart they are extracting every last drop. Research shows people feel better about deals with diminishing concessions than with a single "fair" number — even if they pay more.

**The Mishary rent negotiation:** Mishary's landlord wanted to *raise* his rent. Using the Ackerman model with decreasing nonround offers ($1,730 → $1,790 → $1,810 → $1,829), Mishary ended with a rent *discount* — the landlord moved in the opposite direction from where he started, purely through the psychological pull of diminishing concessions.

**The Farouq dean story:** The dean opened with $300. Farouq dropped an extreme anchor of $1,000. The dean was shocked. They ended up at $750. Farouq had doubled his budget by being willing to go first with a number that seemed absurd. "Any response that is not outright rejection means you have the edge."

**Voss's Toyota 4Runner:** A car deal stuck at $36,000. Voss walked in with his Ackerman sequence pre-planned. He wrote "YOU WIN" on paper with a smiley face and slid it across the table at the end. Walked out at $30,000. The paper was theater; the system was the substance.

### Saying "No" Without the Word (Five Levels)

Use in sequence. Each says "No" deferentially, without triggering confrontation:
1. *"How am I supposed to do that?"* — deferential, invites them to solve your problem
2. *"Your offer is very generous. I'm sorry, that just doesn't work for me."* — the word "generous" nudges them to live up to it; "I'm sorry" builds empathy
3. *"I'm sorry, but I'm afraid I just can't do that."* — the "can't" triggers empathy for your constraints
4. *"I'm sorry, no."* — gently, with downward inflection
5. If needed: *"No."* — calm, not aggressive, last resort

### Negotiating Style Types

Identify your counterpart's type before you bargain. 66% of people have a different "normal" than you. The **"I am normal" paradox** — projecting your own style onto them — is one of the most damaging assumptions in negotiation. A CEO who expected 9/10 of his negotiations to fail was projecting his Analyst detachment onto counterparts who were Accommodators or Assertives. The rule: "treat them the way they *need* to be treated," not the way you would want to be treated.

| Type | Values | Silence means | What works | What to avoid |
|------|--------|--------------|------------|---------------|
| **Analyst** | Preparation, accuracy, no surprises | Thinking (not anger) | Data, warnings, time to process, no ad-libbing. Smile — it opens them up. | Rushing, surprises, emotional pressure |
| **Accommodator** | Relationship, free information flow, emotional connection | Anger — they feel dismissed or disrespected | Sociability, listen first, then implementation questions to move from talk to action | Cutting off their talking; being too task-focused |
| **Assertive** | Time, results, winning | Opportunity to talk more | Mirrors, summaries, getting to "That's right" — they must feel heard before they can hear you | Talking when they want to talk; interrupting; letting their aggression throw you |

**Type-specific silence interpretations matter enormously:**
- Analyst silence = thinking. Don't rush to fill it. Give them time.
- Accommodator silence = something is wrong. Probe gently.
- Assertive silence = they are waiting for you to stop so they can talk more.

**The study of American lawyer-negotiators:** 65% were cooperative, only 24% were assertive. Of those ranked most effective, 75%+ were cooperative. Assertive is counterproductive most of the time. Knowing your own type — and moderating it — is as important as reading the counterpart's.

**Strategic umbrage:** When an offer is genuinely insulting, a controlled expression of *real* (not performed) anger redirects the negotiation. Channel anger at the proposal, not the person: *"I don't see how that would ever work."* Faked anger backfires — counterparts detect it and dig in harder.

**Ready-to-walk mindset:** Never be needy for a deal. If you cannot say "No," you are the counterpart's hostage. Know your walk-away point and be genuinely willing to use it. The person across the table is not the problem; the unsolved issue is. Never create an enemy.

## 11. Find the Black Swans — Unknown Unknowns

Black Swans are pieces of information that, if known, would completely change the negotiation dynamic. They sit outside regular expectations and cannot be predicted from prior experience. Each party typically has at least three.

William Griffin walked into a bank, took hostages, and killed one on deadline. No hostage-taker had ever done that — until he did. The FBI couldn't see the Black Swan because they were filtering everything through known templates: hostage-takers always want something; they always talk; they always negotiate. Griffin wanted to die. Once you know the Black Swan, everything makes sense. Before you know it, the other side looks "crazy."

### Three Types of Leverage

Black Swans are **leverage multipliers**. Leverage is always present — it is an emotional concept that can be manufactured whether it objectively exists or not.

- **Positive leverage:** Your ability to give the counterpart what they want. When they say "I want…" you have positive leverage. Knowing what they desire = knowing what you can withhold or deliver.
- **Negative leverage:** Your ability to make them suffer — threat of harm to reputation, relationships, deal collapse. Use sparingly. "If you shove negative leverage down your counterpart's throat, it might be perceived as taking away their autonomy. People will often sooner die than give up their autonomy." Instead, label it subtly: *"It seems like you strongly value the fact that you've always paid on time."*
- **Normative leverage:** Using the counterpart's own rules, standards, and worldview against them. Find inconsistencies between their stated principles and their actions. No one likes to look like a hypocrite. If they let slip they always pay a certain multiple of cash flow when buying a company, frame your price in that language.

**The paradox of power:** The harder you push, the more likely you are to be met with resistance. That is why negative leverage must be deployed sparingly and subtly.

### Know Their "Religion"

"Religion" means their worldview, moral framework, core identity — whatever authority they defer to: God, the market, the experts, their professional code, their military honor. This is where Black Swans live and where normative leverage is rooted.

The Langer photocopier study confirms the power of connecting to someone's "religion": without a reason, 60% of people let a stranger cut in a line. With a reason — even nonsensical ("because I need to make copies") — 90%+ complied. People respond to reasons that connect to their framework, even when the reason itself has no logical force. Finding and invoking the counterpart's "religion" is the most powerful because-reasoning available.

Dwight Watson, the farmer who drove a tractor into a Washington, D.C., pond: FBI negotiator Winnie Miller noticed subtle Christian references in his speech. She said: "Tell him tomorrow is the Dawn of the Third Day. That's the day Christians believe Jesus Christ ascended to Heaven. If Christ came out on the Dawn of the Third Day, why not Watson?" Watson said "I'll come out" and did. Deep listening to his worldview unlocked the negotiation in 36 hours.

### Three Reasons Counterparts Seem "Crazy"

When behavior doesn't make sense, that is a signal — push into it, don't dismiss it. "They're crazy!" is almost never true.

1. **Ill-informed** — Acting on bad data. Supply correct information. GIGO: Garbage In, Garbage Out.
2. **Constrained** — Cannot do something but won't say why (legal, prior commitments, lack of authority, internal politics). Probe with calibrated questions to surface the constraint.
3. **Hidden interests** — Wants something entirely different from what they're expressing. The refusal is a clue to the real motivation. Griffin didn't want money; he wanted to die. Once you know the real interest, everything makes sense.

### Techniques to Unearth Black Swans

- **Get face time.** Email gives too much time to re-center. Ten minutes in person reveals more than days of research. The Coca-Cola contact admitted he was the wrong person at dinner — something he would never have put in an email.
- **Watch unguarded moments:** the first and last few minutes of meetings (reporters never turn off their recorders), interruptions, off-hand comments, moments when someone breaks from the script. Façades crack at the edges.
- **Review everything you hear.** Use backup listeners whose only job is to listen between the lines. You will miss things. They will hear them.
- **Mislabel intentionally** to get the counterpart to correct you and reveal real information. The real estate student said "it seems like the seller must have doubts about future market fundamentals" — the broker corrected him and revealed that the seller was desperate because of bad mortgages on other properties. That Black Swan dropped the price from $4.3M to $3.6M.
- **Ask "crazy" questions.** The Pittsburgh drug dealer asked "Hey, dog, how do I know she's all right?" — not a scripted proof-of-life question but an open calibrated one that made the kidnapper pause 10 seconds and then volunteer to put the hostage on the phone.
- **The similarity principle:** People trust those who seem similar. Find genuine common ground — shared values, experiences, worldview, even vocabulary. Voss said "stewardship" to a born-again Christian CEO who felt no one understood him. He was hired on the spot.

## 12. Prepare a Negotiation One Sheet

"When the pressure is on, you don't rise to the occasion — you fall to your highest level of preparation."

Before every significant negotiation, prepare five sections. Good initial preparation yields at least a **7:1 return on time invested** — deals don't need to be renegotiated or re-clarified later. Avoid the trap of scripting exact outcomes; over-prepared scripts make you rigid and exploitable. Prepare tools and questions, not predicted transcripts.

**Section 1 — Goal:** Write down your *optimistic but legitimate* best-case outcome. Not your BATNA. The BATNA tricks negotiators into aiming low — once you've spent hours on it, it becomes your mental target and you concede everything beyond it. Research is clear: people who set specific, challenging, but realistic goals get better deals than those who don't set goals or who "try their best." *People who expect more get more.* Discuss your goal with a colleague — this makes it harder to wimp out during the actual negotiation. Carry the written goal into the room.

**Section 2 — Summary:** Two sentences describing the known facts leading to this negotiation — the lay of the land, from both sides' perspectives. You must be able to state this in a way that gets a "That's right" from the counterpart. If they don't say it, you haven't understood their situation.

**Section 3 — Labels / Accusation Audit:** List every accusation they might level at you or your proposal — no matter how unfair or ridiculous. Convert each into a label. Prepare 3–5. Role-play them with a colleague until they feel natural and not self-loathing. It is theater; rehearse it. Fill-in-the-blank templates: *"It seems like ___ is important to you."* / *"It seems you feel like my company is in a unique position to ___."* / *"It seems like you are worried that ___."*

**Section 4 — Calibrated Questions:** Prepare 3–5 "What" and "How" questions designed to uncover hidden motivations, identify behind-the-table players, reveal constraints, and lead toward your goal. Cover three categories:
- *What are we trying to accomplish? / How is that worthwhile? / What's the core issue here?* — general motivations
- *How does this affect the rest of your team? / How on board are the people not on this call? / What do your colleagues see as their main challenges?* — behind-the-table players
- *What are we up against here? / What happens if you do nothing? / What does doing nothing cost you? / How does making this deal resonate with what your company prides itself on?* — deal-killing issues

Be ready to execute follow-up labels after their answers to keep information flowing.

**Section 5 — Noncash Offers:** List things the counterpart possesses that would be valuable to you at low or no cost to them. The Memphis Bar Association gave Voss a magazine cover story; it cost them nothing and was worth a steep discount. Ask: *"What could they give that would almost get me to do it for free?"* A surprising percentage of deals hinge on something outside dollars and cents — self-esteem, status, autonomy, public recognition, access.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- **Splitting the difference.** Always worse than either pure outcome. One black shoe, one brown shoe.
- **Pushing for "Yes" early.** Makes the counterpart defensive; produces counterfeit Yes that commits to nothing.
- **Asking "Why?"** Almost always sounds accusatory. Reframe as "What caused…" or "What made…"
- **Taking deadlines at face value.** They are almost always flexible and seldom carry real consequences.
- **Projecting your own negotiation style onto the counterpart.** Two-thirds of people have a different "normal." Treat them as they need to be treated, not as you want to be treated.
- **Letting ego drive.** If the counterpart congratulates *you*, you did too much — they should be congratulating themselves. The goal is for them to feel the solution was their idea.
- **Treating "You're right" as agreement.** It means "please stop talking." Use calibrated questions until you get "That's right."
- **Faking anger.** Only real, controlled anger works. Strategic umbrage must be genuine. Performed anger backfires and destroys trust.
- **Ignoring behind-the-table players.** One unseen deal-killer destroys agreements. It only takes one.
- **Wimping out on extreme anchors.** The first number sets the whole frame. Aim high (or low). Farouq doubled his budget request by dropping an extreme anchor after absorbing the dean's first punch.
- **BATNA obsession.** Spending hours on your walk-away point makes it your mental target. Aim for your best case, not your worst acceptable case.
- **Making a counteroffer to an extreme low offer.** Counter with "No" sequences instead. Let the other side bid against themselves. Multi-step No sequences routinely move prices 20–30% without you naming a counter number.
- **Scheduling important negotiations for early in a week, or near the beginning of a quarter.** Counterparts are most flexible near their self-imposed deadlines — Fridays, end of month, end of quarter.
- **Treating "wimp-win" as a neutral outcome.** Giving in out of fear is not diplomacy. It produces the worst deals and signals that future concessions are available.
- **Appealing to reason without addressing emotion.** Damasio's research is conclusive: without emotion engaged, reason alone cannot decide. Every purely logical argument that fails does so because the emotional channels were never opened.
